The Beginning
Title & Abstract — What is my research about?
The first thing anyone reads
▾
Think of the title and abstract as your research's shop window — they tell people what's inside before they commit to reading everything.
What is the title saying?
Your title is: "AI-Driven Credit Scoring and Financial Inclusion in Developing Economies: A Cross-Regional Configurational Analysis." In plain English: you are studying how AI tools that decide who gets a loan can help more people in poorer countries access money — and you are comparing 9 countries across Africa, Latin America and Asia to see what conditions make it work.
What does the abstract tell us?
The abstract is a short summary (about half a page) of the whole proposal. It tells the reader: the problem (many people in developing countries can't get loans), what you will do (study 9 countries using a method called fsQCA plus machine learning and a survey), and what you hope to find (which combinations of conditions help AI credit scoring improve financial inclusion).
Key terms in plain English
AI-Driven Credit Scoring
Using computer technology and data (like mobile phone payments) to decide if someone can get a loan — instead of relying on a bank history.
Financial Inclusion
Making sure everyone — especially poor or rural people — has access to financial services like loans, savings and payments.
Developing Economies
Countries that are still growing economically — like Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Brazil, Vietnam etc.
fsQCA
A research method (Fuzzy-Set Qualitative Comparative Analysis) that looks at combinations of conditions rather than single causes. Like asking: what mix of ingredients makes a cake rise?
Configurational Analysis
Looking at how different factors work together — not just one at a time.

Chapter 1
Introduction — Why does this research matter?
Background, rationale, gap, problem, objectives, and research questions
▾
The introduction is you telling your reader: here is the problem, here is why nobody has properly solved it yet, and here is what I am going to do about it.
What is the research background (Section 1.1)?
Millions of people in countries like Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, Brazil and Bangladesh cannot get bank loans because they have no formal credit history. Mobile money has helped with payments, but not with getting actual credit. AI can change this by using things like mobile transaction data and utility bills to decide if someone is creditworthy.
What is the research rationale (Section 1.2)?
This section explains WHY the study is needed. Even though mobile money is growing, access to credit is still very low for poor and rural people. AI offers a solution but also brings risks like bias and privacy problems. The study is also connected to the UN's global goals for poverty reduction and gender equality (SDGs).
What is the research gap (Section 1.3)?
A research gap is what nobody has properly studied yet. Your four gaps are: (1) financial inclusion is still measured the old way, not accounting for AI; (2) most AI credit scoring evidence comes from rich countries, not the 9 you are studying; (3) ethical concerns about AI are not linked to actual inclusion outcomes; (4) AI, regulation and institutions are studied separately, never as one connected system.
What is the problem statement (Section 1.4)?
The problem statement sums up the core challenge: traditional credit scoring fails poor and informal workers. AI could fix this but faces 3 big challenges — ethical risks (bias), innovation vs regulation gaps, and the fact that AI models from rich countries don't always work in poorer ones.
What are the research questions and objectives (Section 1.5)?
Your big question is: Under what combinations of conditions do developing countries achieve higher financial inclusion through AI credit scoring? You have 4 sub-questions and 4 matching objectives — covering the key conditions, regulatory pathways, cross-regional comparison, and data-sparse contexts.
💡 Why 9 countries? You chose 3 countries from each region (Africa, Latin America, Asia) to compare how AI credit scoring works under different conditions — different levels of mobile technology, different regulations, different income levels.

Chapter 2
Literature Review — What has already been studied?
Theories, empirical evidence, and gaps in the field
▾
The literature review shows you have read widely and deeply, and that you know exactly where your research fits in the existing conversation.
What is the theoretical foundation?
You use Bourdieu's theory of capital as your main lens — the idea that people need different types of "capital" (money, skills, networks) to get ahead. You argue that in the digital age, "digital capital" (like having a smartphone, mobile data history, and digital literacy) determines who gets access to credit through AI systems.
What is Bourdieu's theory in plain English?
Pierre Bourdieu was a French sociologist who said success in life depends on the resources you have — not just money, but also education, skills and social connections. In your research, people with more digital activity (mobile payments, internet use) have more "digital capital" and are more likely to get loans from AI systems. People without it are left out — which can make inequality worse.
What are the three supporting theories?
TAM (Technology Acceptance Model) — explains why people adopt or reject new technology. Diffusion of Innovations — explains how technology spreads through society slowly and unevenly. Resource-Based View (RBV) — explains why some institutions (big banks) adopt AI easily while smaller ones can't afford to. Together they explain the human and institutional side of AI adoption.
What does the empirical literature say?
Research from Kenya, Nigeria and Ghana shows AI credit scoring can expand access to loans, but it depends on strong regulation and governance. Evidence from India, China, Brazil and Mexico shows similar results — AI works well when there is good infrastructure and regulation. But in data-poor and informal economies it struggles.

Chapter 3
Research Methodology — How will I do my research?
fsQCA, machine learning, survey — explained simply
▾
The methodology chapter explains your research plan — what tools you will use, how you will collect and analyse data, and why your approach is the right one.
What is the overall research design?
You use a mixed methods design — meaning you combine numbers (data analysis) with real people's experiences (survey). You have 3 components working together: (1) fsQCA as the main analysis, (2) machine learning to validate findings, and (3) a user survey to ground findings in real experience.
What is fsQCA and why use it?
fsQCA (Fuzzy-Set Qualitative Comparative Analysis) looks at combinations of factors rather than single causes. Instead of asking "does regulation cause financial inclusion?" it asks "which combination of regulation + digital access + financial literacy + institutional quality produces financial inclusion?" It is ideal for your 9-country study because it works well with small groups of cases and identifies pathways rather than averages.
What are the 5 conditions you are studying?
1. Digital Financial Ecosystem — how advanced is mobile/internet and fintech in each country?
2. Regulatory Environment — how strong and clear are financial regulations?
3. Financial Literacy — do people understand financial products?
4. Institutional Quality — how trustworthy and effective are government and financial institutions?
5. Market Competition — how many providers are competing to offer credit?
What is calibration in fsQCA?
Calibration means converting raw data into scores between 0 and 1. For example, a country with very high financial inclusion gets a score close to 1, and a country with very low inclusion gets a score close to 0. This allows you to compare all 9 countries on the same scale fairly.
What does machine learning add?
After fsQCA identifies which combinations of conditions work, machine learning (specifically Random Forest) checks which individual conditions are most important across all 9 countries. It also picks up hidden patterns that fsQCA might miss. Think of it as a second opinion that confirms or challenges your main findings.
What is the survey for?
The online survey (200–300 fintech users, ~35–55 per country) asks real users about their experience with AI credit systems. This fills in what secondary databases can't tell you — how people actually feel about regulation, literacy, trust, and access. It validates whether your data-based findings match reality on the ground.
What data sources are you using?
Global Findex (World Bank) — financial inclusion rates. World Governance Indicators — regulatory quality and rule of law. IMF Financial Access Survey — banking access. GSMA Mobile Economy Reports — mobile money data. ITU ICT Database — internet and mobile infrastructure.
💡 Why pragmatist philosophy? You state your research philosophy is pragmatism — meaning you are focused on what works in practice, not proving one universal theory. This justifies using multiple methods together.
Key citations in this chapter

Citations
How to read and use citations
APA format, in-text citations, and what each key source says
▾
Citations are how academics give credit and show evidence. Every claim in your proposal is backed by a source. Here is how to read and use them.
How do in-text citations work?
When you write (Berg et al., 2018) in your text, it means: "this idea is supported by Berg and co-authors in their 2018 paper." The full reference is in the reference list at the end. "et al." means "and others" — used when there are more than 2 authors.
How do I read an APA reference?
Example: Berg, T., Burg, V., Gombović, A., & Puri, M. (2018). On the rise of fintechs — Credit scoring using digital footprints. Review of Financial Studies, 33(7), 2845–2897.
This tells you: Authors (Berg, Burg, Gombović, Puri) · Year (2018) · Title of article · Journal name (Review of Financial Studies) · Volume(Issue) (33(7)) · Page numbers (2845–2897).
